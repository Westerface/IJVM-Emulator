Texas Hold’em: Evaluating the Options

While Artificial Intelligence has been used in two-player zero-sum games such as Chess and Go to create Machine Learning algorithms like Deep Blue [1] and AlphaGo [2] respectively, complex games, like Poker, have not been explored extensively until the 2000s [3, 4, 5]1. Poker is an inherently complicated game [3, 5], because firstly, all the information about the game is not available to a player at any given time, and secondly, finding a solution to the game requires studying thearticle human behaviour closely. In this paper, we2 discuss the merits and demerits of three different approaches that have been applied to solve this problem in the past two decades: making a model of the Poker game [3]3, studying the human behaviour for neural network training [4] and a hybrid of both of the above approaches [5], and hence evaluate their results4.

The approach presented by Schaeffer et al. in their paper “Learning to Play Strong Poker” demonstrates the feasibility of an algorithm, Loki, which is aimed to solve the game of Poker by making a model of the opponent [3]. To solve the problem, Schaeffer et al. start with the same model for all the opponents. As the game progresses, the algorithm analyses the opponents according to their betting strategy and bluffing characteristics, and tries to maintain a table for each opponent which is used for evaluation while the algorithm decides its move. Also, the algorithm tries to play using an unpredictable strategy so that the opponents cannot exploit the weaknesses of the algorithm. Loki has been proven to deliver good results, while being unpredictable [3, p7-8]. The drawback in this approach is that every time the algorithm plays against a new opponent, a fresh model of the opponent is created, hence decreasing the chances of winning till the model has been completed5.

The second approach is presented by Southey et al. in their paper “Bayes’ Bluff: Opponent Modelling in Poker”, which aims to solve the game by providing a more mathematical approach6. The algorithm attempts to solve a simplified version of Texas Hold’em Poker, Leduc Hold’em Poker, by making a Bayesian probabilistic model of the opponent [5, p2]. For a two player game (say between A and B), a decision tree is made, which at first is empty. When the training games are played, the decision trees are expanded: at the top of the tree we have A's private cards and the public cards as they are displayed, and at the bottom we have A and B's private cards, all the public cards and the winning hand. Nowlinking during learning process, many trees could have been made which have the same set of cards while the actions taken by the players A and B were different. These actions are studied and the Dirichlet distribution graph is constructed. Nowlinking the actions which are the most probable to get a better outcome are preferred over the others if the same situation is encountered again by the algorithm while playing the game, which in turn leads to increase in number of wins in the game. This is the ‘Max a posteriori’ strategy [5, p5]. This strategy works really wellformality for Leduc Hold’em Poker. But for this variant of poker, one can list out all the possible combinations of private and public cards in the game, and hence oncemiscellaneous spelling doesn’tformality have to invest extensive time and large samples in training of the algorithm. But7 in Texas Hold'em Poker, the number of possible combination of cards in a game are to the order of 1018, which makes the learning process nearly impossible [5, p8].

Another8 approach to this problem is discussed by Fredrik A. Dahl in his paper “A Reinforcement Learning Algorithm Applied to Simplified Two-Player Texas Hold’em Poker”. This approach tries to combine the approaches that have beenverb tense presented in the above paper [4]. This approachreference clarity, like Southey et al.’s approach [5], uses decision trees to predict the best possible solution for a particular situation, where each node of the tree is a possible outcome of the game. This algorithm is used on a two player Texas Hold'em game. During the learning process, the training game data along with the desired results of the games is given to the algorithm. At every node, the algorithm evaluates all the options up to a particular number of turns and decides which one is the best possible strategy for a particular situation. Then the final result of the game is calculated and is compared to the desired outputs which were given to the algorithm. The algorithm then back-propagates in the decision trees and updates the probability values of the nodes, essentially rewiring the neural network and making it more efficient. This approach, however, requires a large amount of training game data to properly arrange the neural network. Also, the playing style of the algorithm would depend totally upon how the player in the training games tackled different situations9.

The above approaches all have different merits and demerits10. The main requirement to solve the game is that the algorithm should be able to maximize the amount of money won at the games, while trying to alter its strategy so as to not give away hints of its weaknesses to the opponent [3, p211]. While Schaeffer et al.’s approach [3] comes really close to solving the problem in an optimal way, the fact that the algorithm has to learn all over again for a new opponent deters it from completely solving the problem. Southey et al.’s approach [5] is less realistic for Texas Hold’em as the training dataset required would be very large, and the results are still not satisfactory. Lastly, the approach presented by Fredrik A. Dahl [4], though requires large training dataset to get started with solving the game, gives satisfactory results without any major drawbacks, and hence is {*}articlebest of the three approaches provided.
